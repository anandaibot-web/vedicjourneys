translation: >-
  Whenever there is a decline of righteousness, O Arjuna, and a rise

commentary: >-
  This verse is the heart of Krishna's self-declaration in the Gita.
  The word *sṛjāmi* — "I create myself", "I manifest" — is striking.
  Krishna does not say "I come" as though from elsewhere. He says he
  manifests himself, as an actor steps into a role without losing
  their essential nature.

  The verse contains a profound statement about the nature of divine
  action: it is not reactive, not desperate, not interventionist in
  the way of a god who must fix his broken creation. The manifestation
  arises naturally, like heat arises from fire, whenever the conditions
  call for it.

  For Vishishtadvaita, this verse confirms that Vishnu-Narayana takes
  actual form — the avatara is not an illusion but a real, qualified
  descent in the body of prakrti controlled by Brahman. For Advaita,
  the avatara is Brahman's maya-play, appearing as form while remaining
  formless. For Dvaita, it is the literal, personal descending of the
  supreme God, Vishnu, in his full divine majesty.

  All three schools agree on the function: the avatara restores dharma
  not through coercion but through *example* — by living, teaching,
  and acting in a way that reorients the collective moral imagination.
realLifeObservation: >-
  The verse does not specify what dharma is or describe a single type
  of decline. This is deliberate. Dharma is multidimensional — social,
  personal, cosmic. When it frays, the question is not only whether
  a divine figure will appear, but whether a human being will step
  into the gap.

  Many teachers in the Vedantic tradition — Ramakrishna, Vivekananda,
  Ramana Maharshi — have been described by their students in language
  drawn from this verse. Their appearance at particular historical
  moments of spiritual confusion is understood as the same principle
  operating through different channels.

  The deeper personal reading: wherever in your own life dharma — your
  truest nature, your clearest understanding — is in decline, the
  promise is that the inner teacher (the Atman, which the Gita identifies
  with Krishna) will manifest. The question is whether we are quiet
  enough to hear it.

This verse is the first of a famous pair (followed by 4:8 — "for the protection of the good, for the destruction of evil, and for the establishment of righteousness, I appear in every age"). Together they form the classical articulation of the avatara doctrine, and have been cited in every subsequent Vaishnavite theology.
