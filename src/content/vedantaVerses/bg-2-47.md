translation: >-
  You have a right to perform your prescribed duties, but you are not
  entitled to the fruits of your actions. Never consider yourself the
  cause of the results of your activities, and never be attached to
  not doing your duty.

commentary: >-
  This is perhaps the single most quoted verse from the Bhagavad Gita,
  and among the most misread. Krishna is not asking Arjuna to be
  indifferent or robotic. He is making a precise philosophical point
  about ownership and causation.

  The claim "I am the cause of this result" is an ego-appropriation that
  Krishna identifies as the root of bondage. When we act believing "my
  action → my result", we tighten the knot of identity around the doer
  role. The result is suffering: when results don't come as expected,
  the "I" who expected them feels betrayed.

  The teaching is not passive. It is action with full engagement but
  without self-identification as the ultimate author. The fruit belongs
  to the totality of causes, of which your action is one thread.
  This is karma yoga — not the absence of desire but the absence of
  possessiveness over outcomes.

  Shankara reads this verse as supporting the preliminary discipline
  needed for jnana: a person who acts without grasping at results
  purifies the mind, making it fit to receive the teaching of non-duality.
realLifeObservation: >-
  Think of a surgeon in the operating theatre. The best surgeons describe
  entering a state of complete absorption in the procedure itself —
  not thinking about reputation, fees, or outcome. The hands know what
  to do. The mind is clear. That state of action-without-ownership is
  what Krishna is pointing to. It is not coldness; it is the highest
  form of presence.

  Or consider how anxiety corrupts performance. The tennis player who
  is thinking about the championship point misses the championship
  point. The action and the awareness of the action's stake become
  entangled. Krishna's instruction is a kind of radical present-ness:
  do what you are called to do, completely, without the future reward
  colonising the present moment.

This verse appears in the Sankhya Yoga chapter, where Krishna has just told Arjuna that the Self is eternal and bodies come and go. The instruction on action without attachment follows naturally: if the Self is not the body, the Self is not the doer in the absolute sense either. Act from this understanding.
