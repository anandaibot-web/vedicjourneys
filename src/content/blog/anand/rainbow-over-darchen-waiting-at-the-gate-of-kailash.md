## Introduction – Why This Journey

Darchen does not ask to be loved. It sits at the edge of everything — a small clutch of buildings pressed against the vast Tibetan plateau as if the land itself forgot to stop. You do not come here for comfort. You come because something older than reason pulled you across permits and altitude checks and bone-rattling roads, because Mount Kailash is twenty-two kilometres north of where you are standing right now, and that fact alone rearranges the inside of your chest.

I arrived in the afternoon, when the sky was already in conversation with itself — dark cumulonimbus pressing down from the north, the kind of clouds that carry weight you can feel before the first drop falls. Darchen is a waystation in the truest sense: you are neither here nor there, neither arrived nor yet begun. The parikrama — the 52-kilometre clockwise circumambulation of the sacred mountain — starts from this town, and every hour you spend waiting in it is an hour of held breath.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

The plateau here is immense in a way that undoes the eye. Brownish-green grassland stretches to a low mountain range along the southern horizon, the peaks half-swallowed by cloud, no snow on them from this angle, just dark rock dissolving into grey. The light does something unusual in the late afternoon after rain — it breaks through the lower cloud layer in warm orange-red tones just above the horizon line, as if the earth is lit from below rather than above.

Then the rainbow came.

It arced full and diagonal across the entire sky — not a polite half-curve at the edge of vision but a complete, vivid band of colour cutting from upper atmosphere down to the far end of the plateau. Red, orange, yellow, green, blue, indigo, violet — each band distinct, almost aggressive in its clarity against the pewter-grey of the storm behind it. I stood outside and forgot to breathe. The thin air here already makes the lungs work harder; this made them work harder still, for a different reason.

The altitude does something specific to the body that no amount of preparation fully anticipates. My pulse runs a little fast. Each step up a small incline asks for a deliberate breath. The cold has an edge that cuts through whatever you are wearing — dry and sharp, nothing like the damp cold of lower elevations. The wind off the plateau is not gusty; it is continuous, a low, steady pressure against the left side of my face. Electricity pylons run east across the flatland, their towers vanishing into the curtain of distant rain. The rain itself is visible on the left horizon — a grey smear hanging below the clouds, not yet here but coming.

I watched the rainbow hold its position across all of it, indifferent to the storm it was born from.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

There is a teaching I keep returning to on this journey — that the witness and the witnessed are not two things. The rainbow appears in the sky; the sky does not become the rainbow. The storm passes; the sky does not grieve it. I have been thinking about this since we walked through Yama Dwar, the gate that marks the formal beginning of the Kailash parikrama, where the mountain first declared itself fully — but here in Darchen, waiting, the teaching becomes sharper.

I am watching the rainbow. Something in me insists on calling it beautiful, on claiming the experience as mine, on filing it under *things that happened to me in Tibet.* But the seeing itself — the bare fact of light entering and the knowing of colour — that has no owner. It simply occurs. The plateau does not perform for the pilgrim. The storm does not care about the parikrama. The rainbow arcs across its own logic entirely.

What dissolves at this altitude, slowly, is the insistence that I am the centre of what I am perceiving. That loosening is not dramatic. It is quiet, like air slowly leaving a room.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** यथा दीपो निवातस्थो नेङ्गते सोपमा स्मृता। योगिनो यतचित्तस्य युञ्जतो योगमात्मनः॥

**Transliteration:** Yathā dīpo nivātastho neṅgate sopamā smṛtā | Yogino yatacittasya yuñjato yogam ātmanaḥ ||

**Meaning:** As a lamp in a windless place does not flicker — that is the simile used for the disciplined mind of the yogi engaged in the yoga of the Self.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 6, Verse 19

**Relevance:** Standing on the plateau in Darchen, the wind is unceasing and the sky is violent with weather — yet the task of the parikrama is precisely this: to find the still point inside the crossing storm. The rainbow does not flinch. The yogi's mind, the Gita says, should not either. At this altitude, in this wind, that instruction feels less metaphorical than usual.

## Practical Travel Notes

Darchen sits at roughly 4,560 metres and serves as the base camp for the Kailash parikrama. To reach it, you need a Tibet Travel Permit, an Alien Travel Permit, and a separate Kailash restricted area permit — all arranged through a registered Chinese travel agency; independent travel here is not permitted. The standard approach is via Lhasa by air or train, then overland through Shigatse and Ali (Ngari prefecture) — a journey of several days.

July is within the main pilgrimage season (May through September), which offers the most stable weather and open roads — though afternoon thunderstorms are common and the plateau sky changes fast, as I learned. Acclimatise seriously: spend at least two nights in Lhasa before proceeding, and ideally one night in Shigatse. Do not rush the altitude gain.

Essential gear includes windproof and waterproof outer layers, strong hiking boots, trekking poles for the Drolma La pass at 5,630 metres, and a reliable sleeping bag rated below freezing. Guesthouses in Darchen are basic — hot water is intermittent, electricity can cut out. Carry cash; card payments are unreliable at this end of the plateau.

## Closing Reflection

The rainbow was gone within twenty minutes. The storm closed back over where it had been, and the plateau returned to its usual grey-brown indifference. What stayed was not the colour — it was the reminder that the most vivid things here have the shortest tenure. Kailash does not negotiate. The mountain will be there tomorrow at the start of the parikrama, exactly as it has been for ten thousand years before anyone thought to walk around it. That scale is not inspiring. It is simply true. And standing in Darchen, in the cold, watching the last light thin out along the horizon, true is exactly enough.
