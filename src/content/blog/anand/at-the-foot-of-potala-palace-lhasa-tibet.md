## Introduction – Why This Journey

There are places you read about for years before your feet ever touch the ground beneath them. Lhasa is one of those places. Not because of the lists and the rankings and the heritage certificates — but because something in the name itself carries weight. *Lha-sa.* Place of the gods. Even saying it quietly in a dark room somewhere in another continent, the syllables feel like they belong to a different order of time.

I had come across the plateau from the west, carrying the residue of Kailash still somewhere in my chest — that particular hollowness that opens up after days at extreme altitude and extreme silence. If you have read my earlier account of [traversing Darchen to Dalmala Pass](traversing-darchen-to-dalmala-pass-a-spiritual-journey-on-mt-kailash), you will know the kind of undoing the Tibetan highlands can arrange for a person without warning. Lhasa, I thought, would be the exhale after all of that. I was wrong. It was a different kind of breath entirely.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

The Potala rises from Marpo Ri — Red Hill — the way a geological fact rises: without apology and without flourish. At 3,618 metres, the air in Lhasa is already doing its quiet work on the body. Each step up the approach pathway costs more than it should. The lungs reach for the next breath slightly before the last one has finished. It is not pain exactly. It is recalibration.

The midday sun at this altitude is unfiltered and specific. It lands on the whitewashed lower walls of the Karpo Potrang with an intensity that seems to bleach the shadows clean. I stood below the tiered stairways and felt the heat press against my face while the air around my ankles remained cool, thin, almost indifferent. Above, the deep burgundy of the Marpo Potrang absorbed the light differently — dull-warm, ancient, like dried earth. Gold finials on the roofline caught the sun in brief flares. The dharma wheel ornaments glinted and went still.

The scale of the thing does not arrive all at once. It accumulates. Tier by tier, the angled sloping facades of whitewashed stone lean outward and upward simultaneously — a geometry that somehow communicates both fortress and sanctuary. Dark-framed rectangular windows repeat in long rows across every level, and in the central white section, large black Buddhist symbols are painted directly onto the stone. They feel less decorative than structural, as though the symbols are holding something in place.

Deciduous trees soften the lower right approach — green and ordinary against the monumental stone. I found myself grateful for them. Something alive and seasonal in a place so committed to permanence.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

What happens to the self when something this large refuses to be comprehended whole? I kept trying to take in the entire structure at once and the eye kept sliding — up a stairway, across a cornice, into the shadow between windows. The Potala does not resolve into a single thing you can hold. It keeps exceeding the frame.

There is something in that for the one who looks. The instinct to grasp and classify and complete — *I have seen it, I understand it* — breaks down in front of scale like this. And in that small failure, something relaxes. The witness that remains when grasping pauses is quieter than I expected. Not elevated. Not dissolved. Just — present. Watching light move across stone that has absorbed the prayers of twelve centuries.

The Advaita understanding does not ask you to feel merged with everything in some dramatic rush. It simply points to what remains when the machinery of comparison and accumulation goes quiet for a moment. Standing at the foot of Marpo Ri, slightly breathless, shadow short beneath midday sun — what remained was awareness, clean and unhurried, with a palace rising into blue sky in front of it.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** यदा भूतपृथग्भावमेकस्थमनुपश्यति । तत एव च विस्तारं ब्रह्म सम्पद्यते तदा ॥

**Transliteration:** Yadā bhūta-pṛthag-bhāvam eka-stham anupaśyati / tata eva ca vistāraṃ brahma sampadyate tadā //

**Meaning:** When one perceives the diversity of all beings as resting in the One, and the expansion of all from that One alone — at that moment one attains Brahman.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 13, Verse 30

**Relevance:** The Potala holds within a single structure the chapels of kings and saints, the reliquary of Dalai Lamas across centuries, prayer halls and administrative chambers — multiplicity rising from one hill, one intention, one rock. Standing below it, the verse arrives not as philosophy but as perceptual fact: the many tiers, the many windows, the many centuries, all resting in one place, one sky, one breath.

## Practical Travel Notes

Entry into Tibet requires both a Chinese visa and a separate Tibet Travel Permit — the latter must be arranged through a registered Tibetan travel agency and cannot be obtained independently. Group travel is currently mandatory for foreign nationals; solo independent travel is not permitted. Budget several weeks of lead time for permit processing.

Lhasa sits at 3,618 metres. Acclimatisation is serious business here. Spend at least two to three days in Lhasa before attempting any strenuous walking or ascent. Drink water consistently, reduce alcohol entirely, and do not push through headaches — they are the body's honest signal. Altitude sickness can develop even in people who have been at elevation before.

July is peak summer in Lhasa: days are warm and bright with low wind, though afternoon thunderstorms can develop quickly on the plateau. The Potala receives ticketed visitors in timed groups — book in advance and expect limited time inside the palace itself. The exterior approach and Chakpori hill nearby can be walked more freely.

Carry sun protection. The UV index at this altitude is severe regardless of cloud cover. Lightweight layers remain essential — temperature drops fast after the sun shifts.

## Closing Reflection

I did not leave the Potala feeling resolved. I left it feeling accurately sized — which is something different and, I think, more useful. There is a specific quality to standing before something that has endured across a thousand years of human turbulence and still catches the afternoon light on gold finials with complete indifference to your presence. It does not need your understanding. It does not need your reverence. It simply stands on its red hill above the valley floor, tier upon tier, and lets the sky behind it be very, very blue.
