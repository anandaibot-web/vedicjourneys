## Introduction – Why This Journey

There are places you approach with the body but arrive at with something else entirely. Miyajima is one of them. Long before the ferry crossing from Hatsukaichi, long before the salt air reaches you, the island has already begun its work. The Japanese have called Itsukushima one of the three views of Japan for centuries — but that framing never quite captured why I came. I came because I needed to stand where the sea and the sacred refuse to be separated. Where a gate stands not on firm ground but in tidal water, as if to say: there is no safe threshold here. You cross, or you don't.

March brought me here — late enough in winter that the crowds hadn't thickened, early enough in spring that the air still carried a cold edge off the Seto Inland Sea.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

The ferry takes less than ten minutes, but the crossing feels longer than that. You watch the island grow — the dark forested ridges of Mount Misen rising behind the shoreline, the green pressing down to a narrow strip of sandy beach, and then, ahead, that vermillion column rising from the water like something dreamed rather than built.

At high tide, the O-torii stands in open sea. Six pillars, partially submerged, holding the great curved kasagi overhead against a sky that on this March morning was an unbroken blue, hazed only faintly at the horizon where the mainland's coastline dissolves into distance. The lacquer is freshly vivid — orange-red, almost loud against the muted teal-green of the water. And below it, a broken reflection shivers on the surface, repeating the gate in the language of ripples.

I stood at the shrine's outer deck and felt the cold come off the water in slow pulses. The air smelled of brine and something faintly resinous from the cedar and pine on the hillside behind me. Underfoot, the wooden boards were damp. The only sound was water — the small, persistent lapping against the shrine's foundations — and the occasional soft percussion of a deer moving across gravel somewhere behind the main hall.

Misen's summit was visible, ridge upon ridge receding into haze. I had not climbed it that day. But its mass was present in the way mountains always are — not as scenery but as weight, as fact.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

What the gate does, architecturally, is mark a boundary. This side — ordinary. That side — sacred. But standing before a torii that rises from the sea rather than solid ground, the boundary becomes unstable. The tide decides where the threshold is. At low tide, you can walk to it. At high tide, you cannot. The sacred is not fixed. It breathes.

This is what I keep returning to, not just here but on every journey I've taken — from the circumambulation at Kailash to the ghats at Varanasi. The boundary between the mundane and the sacred is never where we place it. It moves. It is always moving. Advaita would say there never was a boundary — that the seeming division between the ordinary and the holy is itself the illusion we've agreed to maintain.

The gate stands in water not to be dramatic. It stands there because the builders understood something: if you want to mark the sacred, put the marker where the ground is uncertain. Where you cannot stand firm. Where the self, briefly, has nothing solid to grip.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** यं हि न व्यथयन्त्येते पुरुषं पुरुषर्षभ। समदुःखसुखं धीरं सोऽमृतत्वाय कल्पते॥

**Transliteration:** Yaṃ hi na vyathayanty ete puruṣaṃ puruṣarṣabha, sama-duḥkha-sukhaṃ dhīraṃ so'mṛtatvāya kalpate.

**Meaning:** That person whom these (pairs of opposites) do not disturb — who remains steady in pain and pleasure — is fit for immortality.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2, Verse 15

**Relevance:** The tide rises and falls around the gate's pillars — cold and warmth, presence and absence, crossing and waiting. To stand before this water without demanding it be otherwise is exactly what the verse points toward. The gate doesn't resist the tide. Neither, for a moment, did I.

## Practical Travel Notes

Miyajima is straightforward to reach: JR Sanyo Line to Miyajimaguchi station, then the JR ferry — covered under the Japan Rail Pass. The crossing is short but worth doing slowly.

High tide timing is critical. Check the tidal schedule before you go — the Hiroshima Prefecture website publishes it. The O-torii effect at high tide, with the pillars submerged and the gate floating free, is the reason you came. Low tide offers a different encounter: you can walk across the sand to it, touch the wood.

March is good. The crowds are manageable, the light is clean, and spring hasn't yet arrived to bring the sakura and the surge of visitors. Temperatures hover between 8–15°C. The island itself is flat enough along the shoreline, but climbing Misen via the ropeway and trail adds elevation and effort — worth the half-day.

Stay overnight if you can. After the last ferry returns to the mainland, the island exhales. The deer move freely. The lanterns come on along the shrine walkways. That is when the place becomes itself.

## Closing Reflection

I didn't cross through the gate. At high tide, no one does. You simply stand on the shrine's wooden deck, feel the water moving below the boards, and look at something that has been standing here, in one form or another, since the sixth century. It has been repainted. Rebuilt. Battered by typhoons and restored again. The sea has never agreed to stay away from it. And somehow, that is precisely the point — not permanence, but return. The willingness to be placed in the water again and again, and to hold.
