## Introduction – Why This Journey

There is no rational answer to why a person comes here. The road is brutal, the air is thin to the point of insult, and the bureaucracy of permits alone could break a lesser resolve. And yet, something pulls. Not curiosity. Not the desire to collect a difficult place. Something older than that — a nagging sense that this mountain holds a question the rest of the world is too loud to let you hear.

Gang Rinpoche. The Precious One of Snow. Every tradition that has ever stood before it — Hindu, Buddhist, Jain, Bön — agrees on at least this much: that something here is not ordinary. I did not come as a believer in any fixed doctrine. I came the way a person walks toward the source of a sound they cannot yet identify. Drawn. Uncertain. Alert.

By the time I reached the valley that opens onto the south face, I had already passed through Darchen and crossed the early terrain of the parikrama. The body had begun to understand what the mind had agreed to.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

The south face of Kailash catches the morning sun like nothing else on this plateau. The rock — dark, almost charcoal in shadow — ignites into orange and gold where the light strikes, and vertical ribbons of ice run down its face like pale scars. There is no softness here. The surrounding ridgelines are bare grey stone, no vegetation, no colour except what the sky and the mountain choose to give.

And the sky here is its own thing entirely. At this altitude, the blue is so saturated, so depthless, it feels less like atmosphere and more like the absence of atmosphere — a direct look into something that does not end. The sun is fierce and close. My skin knew it before my eyes adjusted.

I stopped walking. Partly because I had to. My lungs were pulling at air that kept refusing to fill them completely — that familiar plateau sensation of breathing just slightly behind where you need to be. Each step on the gravelly, arid ground required a moment of renegotiation with my own body. The cold moved through everything. Not dramatically, but persistently. The kind of cold that doesn't announce itself — it simply occupies you.

Beside me, a yak stood with the patient authority of something utterly at home. Long dark coat, wide horns, a white-patched face that turned toward the mountain with what I can only describe as indifference — the indifference of a creature that has never needed the mountain to mean anything. It carried its load, decorated with red tassels and the worn geometry of a striped pack blanket, and breathed the thin air without complaint. I envied it that.

In the valley below, flat-roofed white structures — a small monastery, a handful of shelters — sat at the mountain's base with the quiet of things that have accepted their purpose. No drama. Just presence.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

I had written something in an earlier entry — standing at Yama Dwar, the gate of Kailash — about the feeling that the parikrama was not a journey around something but into something. Standing here, watching the south face burn with morning light while my breath lagged and my legs ached, I began to understand what I meant by that.

The mountain does not perform. It does not offer comfort. It simply is — so completely, so without apology, that standing before it produces a strange effect: the sense that the one standing is slightly less solid than assumed. Not frightening. Just clarifying.

Advaita asks us to locate the witness — the awareness that watches thought, sensation, effort, and fear arise and pass. Here, the mountain becomes a useful mirror. It does not change. I change. My breath labours; the mountain is still. My legs protest; the mountain is still. Even the emotion that rose in my chest — some wordless pressure between awe and grief — arose and moved through, while the peak above remained exactly what it was.

The ego, at altitude, loses some of its insistence. There is simply not enough oxygen to maintain its usual performance.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** यो मां पश्यति सर्वत्र सर्वं च मयि पश्यति। तस्याहं न प्रणश्यामि स च मे न प्रणश्यति॥

**Transliteration:** Yo māṁ paśyati sarvatra sarvaṁ ca mayi paśyati | Tasyāhaṁ na praṇaśyāmi sa ca me na praṇaśyati ||

**Meaning:** One who sees Me everywhere and sees all things in Me — I am never lost to them, nor are they ever lost to Me.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 6, Verse 30

**Relevance:** Before this mountain, the instruction is literal. When the eye stops separating the perceiver from the perceived — when the yak, the ice-streaked rock face, the thin air, and the aching body are all allowed to be one unbroken field — something in the verse stops being philosophy and starts being description. Kailash does not invite worship from a distance. It insists on dissolution of it.

## Practical Travel Notes

The Kailash parikrama requires both a Chinese visa and a Tibet Travel Permit, plus an Alien Travel Permit for restricted areas. These are obtained through registered tour operators — independent travel to this region is not permitted for foreign nationals. Plan for significant lead time.

The best window is late May through mid-September. June and July offer the most stable weather, though afternoon conditions can shift quickly. Avoid attempting this without at minimum two weeks of altitude acclimatisation — Lhasa first, then gradual ascent westward toward Darchen.

The full 52-kilometre parikrama typically takes three days. The Dölma La pass at approximately 5,630 metres is the hardest single point. Yaks are available for hire to carry packs; your lungs will thank you for the decision. Carry altitude sickness medication and know the symptoms. Diamox is commonly used but consult your physician beforehand.

Darchen is the base town. Accommodation is basic. Bring a good sleeping bag, quality trekking poles, and more layers than you think you need.

## Closing Reflection

The yak turned its face back to the trail eventually, and I followed. The mountain did not acknowledge either of us. That, I think, is the point. Kailash offers no reassurance, no sign, no reward for having arrived. What it offers is simpler and harder: a place so indifferent to human need that the human, standing in it long enough, stops needing to be anything other than what is already here. The cold. The breath. The light on rock. Enough.
