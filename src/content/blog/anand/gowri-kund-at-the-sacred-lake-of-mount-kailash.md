## Introduction – Why This Journey

There is no rational explanation for why a person crosses half a continent to stand on a scree slope above five thousand metres, lungs half-empty, legs past argument. I had read about Gowri Kund in fragments — a glacial lake tucked into the flank of the Kailash parikrama just below Dolma La, sacred to Parvati, cold enough to stop a thought mid-sentence. But reading about a place and being pulled toward it are different events entirely. The pull, when it came, was quiet and persistent, the way thirst works. I had written elsewhere about crossing Yama Dwar, that first gateway into the Kailash circuit, and something in that crossing planted a seed I couldn't ignore. Gowri Kund was what waited on the other side of the highest point of the parikrama. I needed to know what the body feels when it arrives somewhere the mind has long considered unreachable.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

The terrain leading to Gowri Kund is not gentle with you. The path — if you can call loose scree a path — demands every gram of attention. Each step lands on a different angle. Ankles negotiate. Knees quietly protest. The air at this altitude carries just enough oxygen to keep you moving but not enough to let you forget you are working. I stopped every twenty steps on the steeper sections, not from weakness but from arithmetic: the body simply requires it here.

What strikes you first, before the lake comes into view, is the prayer flags. They are everywhere — draped over boulders, strung between rock cairns, layered so thickly across the foreground stones that the grey of the mountain almost disappears beneath them. Red, blue, green, yellow, white — the colours are violent against the monochrome of the scree. They snap in the wind with a dry, papery crack, a sound that is both restless and oddly settling, like a mantra repeated until it loses its edges. Countless hands tied these flags here. Countless intentions flew off them into the thin air.

Then the lake appears.

It is the colour that undoes you. A turquoise so saturated and milky — glacial flour suspended in meltwater — that it reads almost artificial against the grey rock surrounding it. No vegetation. No softness anywhere in the landscape. Just cliff face, boulder, sky, and this impossible bowl of colour sitting quietly in the middle of it all. The vertical rock walls on either side rise into jagged spires, grey and indifferent. The sky above is partly clouded, with blue breaking through in patches, the light shifting in slow passes across the water's surface.

I stood at the edge and felt the cold radiating off the water before I touched it. When I did touch it — one palm flat on the surface — the cold moved up my arm and settled somewhere behind the sternum. It was not unpleasant. It was clarifying in the way that only shock can clarify.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

There is a particular quality of stillness that extreme altitude imposes. The mind, usually crowded, thins out like the air itself. At Gowri Kund, standing on those rocks with the wind pulling at me and the lake perfectly unmoved by any of it, I noticed something that is difficult to name without sounding theatrical: the witness was more obvious than usual.

Advaita Vedanta speaks of the sakshi — the unchanging witness behind all experience. In ordinary life, that witness is buried under the noise of preference and planning. Here, with the body reduced to its basic negotiations — breathe, step, balance, breathe — the witness becomes harder to miss. The lake did not care that I was there. The cliff faces had no opinion about my pilgrimage. The prayer flags were not performing for me. Everything was simply what it was, and I was simply what I was, and the boundary between those two facts felt, for a moment, negotiable.

This is not a peaceful feeling, exactly. It is more like being seen through.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** *नित्यः सर्वगतः स्थाणुरचलोऽयं सनातनः*

**Transliteration:** *Nityaḥ sarva-gataḥ sthāṇur acalo'yaṁ sanātanaḥ*

**Meaning:** *This Self is eternal, all-pervading, unchanging, immovable, and ancient beyond reckoning.*

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2, Verse 24

**Relevance:** At Gowri Kund, where the rock has stood through every season and the glacial water holds its colour regardless of who arrives at its edge, the unchanging nature of awareness becomes something you feel rather than merely understand. The verse does not describe a concept here — it describes the quality of the silence between one laboured breath and the next.

## Practical Travel Notes

Gowri Kund lies on the inner Kailash parikrama circuit in Tibet, accessible from Darchen. The full parikrama is approximately 52 kilometres and most pilgrims complete it in three days, though the physically demanding second day — which crosses Dolma La at roughly 5,630 metres and descends past Gowri Kund — is the crux of the entire journey.

**Permits:** Indian nationals require a Kailash Mansarovar Yatra permit through the Ministry of External Affairs or authorised operators. Foreign nationals need a Tibet Travel Permit plus an Alien Travel Permit. Begin this paperwork at least four to six months in advance.

**Best season:** May to early June, and September. July and August bring the monsoon to the southern approaches and can make the route unstable.

**Altitude preparation:** Spend a minimum of two nights in Darchen (approximately 4,560 m) before beginning the parikrama. Acclimatise in Lhasa or Kathmandu beforehand. Carry acetazolamide only if prescribed. Symptoms of altitude sickness here are not to be managed with willpower.

**Gear essentials:** Layered thermals, windproof outer shell, trekking poles — non-negotiable on scree descent — high-altitude sleeping bag rated to minus ten, and water purification. The cold at Gowri Kund on a clear afternoon is still serious.

**Logistics:** Most pilgrims use Darchen as a base. Yak support for luggage is available and advisable. Do not underestimate the descent from Dolma La — loose rock and altitude fatigue make it technically harder than the ascent.

## Closing Reflection

I did not leave Gowri Kund feeling completed. I left it feeling accurately sized — which is a different thing. The lake was indifferent to my arrival and indifferent to my departure, and somewhere in that indifference was something that felt more honest than most of what passes for meaning in ordinary life. The prayer flags will keep snapping in the wind long after the last pilgrim of this season has descended. The water will hold its colour. The cliffs will not remember any of us. And yet we keep coming. Perhaps that is the most truthful thing about pilgrimage — not what it gives you, but what it quietly takes back.
