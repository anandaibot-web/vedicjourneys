## Introduction – Why This Journey

There are places you read about for years before your feet ever touch the ground beneath them. Lhasa is one of those. Not because it promises anything — no brochure language drew me here — but because something in the accumulated weight of human devotion across centuries creates a kind of gravity. You feel it long before you arrive. By the time I had completed the kora around Mount Kailash and descended through Darchen, I understood that Lhasa was not a destination so much as a continuation. The Potala was waiting the way certain truths wait — patient, immovable, indifferent to whether you are ready.

I arrived carrying the tiredness of high passes in my legs and something quieter in my chest. That quietness, I would discover, was exactly the right preparation.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

At 3,618 metres, Lhasa sits lower than the passes I had already crossed, yet the body does not forget where it is. The air here is thin in a way that becomes ordinary after a few days — you simply learn to breathe more deliberately, the way you learn to speak carefully in a language not your own. Each step up the tiered stone staircases that ascend the Potala's face demands that deliberateness. I paused often. Not from weakness, but because the pausing felt right.

The midsummer light in Lhasa is extraordinary — intense and shadowless at noon, then slanting in the afternoon to carve deep shadows along the whitewashed stone walls. The palace's base is massive, sloping outward like the roots of something that grew here rather than was built here. The whitewash catches the sun and throws it back hard. I had to narrow my eyes. Above the white, the upper sections rise in deep maroon, the colour of dried pomegranate, row after row of black-framed trapezoidal windows facing outward like closed eyes. Gold finials crown the roofline — dharma wheels catching the high-altitude light, burning quietly against a sky so deeply blue it seems less like sky and more like depth itself.

The air smells of juniper smoke and warm stone. Somewhere below, green deciduous trees soften the base of the hill with a gentleness that feels almost apologetic against so much stone and authority. Prayer and permanence — both are present simultaneously in the body's experience of this place.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

Standing at the base of those ascending staircases, I noticed something familiar — the same thing I had felt at the foot of Kailash, described in an earlier entry. The self that had traveled here, with its opinions and itineraries and carefully maintained sense of being someone on a meaningful journey, became temporarily embarrassed by the scale of what it was looking at.

The Potala does not ask to be admired. It simply persists. Thirteen stories of human intention compressed into stone over centuries, and yet the overwhelming sensation is not of human achievement but of something that used human hands without particularly needing them. Advaita calls this *Brahman* — the ground that underlies all forms, using them and discarding them with equal equanimity.

When I sat on the lower steps and let my breath slow, the boundary between the one observing and the thing observed became genuinely unclear. Not poetically unclear — actually unclear. The whitewashed wall did not care that I was there. The sky did not care. And in that mutual indifference, something relaxed in me that I had not known was held tight. The witness remained. Everything else negotiated its edges.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** न जायते म्रियते वा कदाचिन् नायं भूत्वा भविता वा न भूयः। अजो नित्यः शाश्वतोऽयं पुराणो न हन्यते हन्यमाने शरीरे॥

**Transliteration:** Na jāyate mriyate vā kadācin nāyaṃ bhūtvā bhavitā vā na bhūyaḥ। Ajo nityaḥ śāśvato'yaṃ purāṇo na hanyate hanyamāne śarīre॥

**Meaning:** It is never born, nor does it die at any time. It has not come into being, does not come into being, and will not come into being. It is unborn, eternal, ever-existing, and primeval. It is not slain when the body is slain.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2, Verse 20

**Relevance:** The Potala is built around the certainty of impermanence — each Dalai Lama came and went, and the palace absorbed them all without loss. Standing here, in a body that laboured with thin air and aching joints, this verse arrived not as consolation but as plain description. What laboured was the body. What watched the golden finials catch the afternoon light was something that had never moved.

## Practical Travel Notes

Entry to Tibet requires a Tibet Travel Permit in addition to a Chinese visa — these are obtained through a registered travel agency and cannot be arranged independently. Allow three to four weeks for processing. Foreign nationals must book through an authorized tour operator; independent travel is not permitted.

July is generally a good month for Lhasa — summer brings warmth and the sky is often clear, though afternoon thunderstorms are possible in the monsoon fringe. Budget two full days in Lhasa before ascending to higher altitudes; altitude sickness is real at 3,618m and symptoms can appear even in people who are otherwise fit.

For the Potala specifically: timed entry tickets are required and numbers are limited daily. Book through your tour operator well in advance. The ascent of the stairways is genuinely demanding — take it at half your normal pace. Carry water, sunscreen, and lip balm; the UV intensity at altitude is aggressive.

If you are continuing onward toward Kailash, factor in a minimum of three acclimatization days in Lhasa. The road west to Darchen climbs significantly, and your body will need the buffer.

## Closing Reflection

I did not leave Lhasa feeling completed. That is the honest truth of it. The Potala gave me nothing I could carry — no insight wrapped neatly, no confirmed belief. What it gave instead was a sustained encounter with something that has been here far longer than I have and will remain far longer than I will. There is a kind of relief in that, once you stop requiring it to mean something on your terms. The gold wheels at the roofline turned in the wind. The sky held its colour. I descended the steps slowly, breathing with intention, and the stone was warm under my hand.
