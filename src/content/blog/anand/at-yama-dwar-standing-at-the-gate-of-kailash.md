## Introduction – Why This Journey

There is no rational explanation for wanting to come here. Yama Dwar — the Gate of Death — is not a destination that lures with comfort or convenience. The road to this threshold crosses some of the most unforgiving terrain on earth, and the name itself is not metaphor. It is instruction. You pass through the gate of Yama, the god of death, before you begin the circumambulation of Kailash. The ancient rishis were not being dramatic. They were being precise.

I had read about this gate long before I ever thought it reachable. The parikrama of Kailash is not simply a trek around a mountain. It is an acknowledgment — that something in you must be surrendered before you complete the circle. I had circled many things in my life without surrendering anything. Perhaps that is why I kept returning to this in my mind, year after year, until July 2025, when I finally stood here.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

The plateau opens up in a way that surprises you even when you've been warned. After the tight valley approaches and the grinding altitude gain from Darchen, Yama Dwar sits in a wide, spare bowl of land that feels deliberately emptied. The grass is thin and pale, clinging to the rocky soil with the kind of determination that shames you a little. The ridgelines above are bare and rust-coloured, layered like sediment in old bone.

The chorten here — white-plastered, banded in deep red and blue and yellow at its base, crowned with a gold spire — stands without apology against this emptiness. It is not ornate by the standards of lower temples, but at this altitude, against this sky, it reads as extravagant. Prayer flags fan outward from poles across the plateau, not fluttering so much as straining, pulled taut by a wind that has no patience for ceremony. The mani stones to one side are carved deep with script that the wind has been polishing for decades. You don't need to read them. Their weight is enough.

My lungs at this altitude are doing their own pilgrimage — reaching for oxygen that arrives in smaller portions than the body is accustomed to. Each breath is a negotiation. The cold is dry and immediate, sitting on the skin of the face like a held compress. The light is diffused through cumulus clouds that seem stacked too close overhead, the sky a deeper blue than logic suggests. The sun, when it breaks through, is without warmth but with tremendous brightness.

I stood near the chorten and felt the ground underfoot — hard-packed, stony, unyielding. There is something clarifying about ground that does not give. It tells you where you are.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

Yama Dwar asks a direct question. Not philosophically — physically. When your chest tightens and each step costs something, the habitual chatter of the thinking mind loses its budget. There is simply not enough air to sustain the usual commentary.

What remains is quieter and less flattering. Not peace — more like exposure. The Advaita understanding that the witness and the witnessed are not two separate things becomes less abstract when the body is stripped down to its essentials. The one who is struggling to breathe and the one who notices the struggle — at this altitude, that gap closes faster than in any meditation hall.

Kailash itself is not visible from Yama Dwar — the mountain reveals itself slowly, in stages, as the parikrama progresses. But its gravity is felt. This is precisely the teaching: the source doesn't need to be visible to be operative. In my earlier writing on the path from Darchen toward Dalmala Pass, I noted how the mountain instructs through absence as much as through presence. Yama Dwar deepens that instruction. The gate is not the destination. Passing through it honestly is the practice.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** नैनं छिन्दन्ति शस्त्राणि नैनं दहति पावकः। न चैनं क्लेदयन्त्यापो न शोषयति मारुतः॥

**Transliteration:** *Nainaṁ chindanti śastrāṇi nainaṁ dahati pāvakaḥ, na cainaṁ kledayantyāpo na śoṣayati mārutaḥ.*

**Meaning:** Weapons cannot cleave it, fire cannot burn it, water cannot wet it, wind cannot dry it.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2, Verse 23

**Relevance:** Standing at the gate named for death, this verse arrives not as consolation but as orientation. The body labours visibly at this altitude — its limits are undeniable — and yet something in the standing here does not labour at all. Yama Dwar is the threshold where that distinction becomes briefly, bodily clear.

## Practical Travel Notes

The Kailash Mansarovar Yatra requires an Inner Line Permit for the Tibet Autonomous Region, typically arranged through the Chinese embassy via designated tour operators. Indian nationals travel via the Lipulekh Pass route or through Kathmandu; permits and logistics differ accordingly. Processing time can extend to several months — begin paperwork no later than January for a June–July window.

July is within the viable season window, though afternoon rain and sudden cloud cover are common. The pre-monsoon weeks of late May to mid-June are drier but colder at night. Avoid the parikrama outside May–September entirely.

Altitude acclimatisation is non-negotiable. Spend a minimum of two nights in Kathmandu, additional nights in Nyalam and Saga before reaching Darchen. Ascend slowly. Diamox is useful but not a substitute for time. Symptoms of AMS — headache, nausea, disorientation — must be taken seriously. Descent is always the correct response to worsening symptoms.

Gear essentials: layered thermals, windproof outer shell, trekking poles (invaluable on the rocky descents), high-SPF lip balm and sunscreen, and water purification. The parikrama itself — 52 kilometres — is typically completed in three days, with the Drolma La pass at 5,636 metres as the physical and psychological crux.

## Closing Reflection

Yama Dwar does not feel sacred in the way that temple courtyards do — with incense and priests and the warmth of accumulated prayer. It feels sacred the way exposed rock faces feel sacred: by being exactly and only what it is. The chorten stands. The flags pull at their ropes. The wind moves through without asking permission.

I passed through the gate carrying everything I came with. That may be the point — to notice, clearly and without embellishment, what you are actually carrying. The mountain beyond does not offer to take it from you. It simply lets you walk around it, and see.
