## Introduction – Why This Journey

You do not choose Varanasi. It chooses you — and usually at a moment when you are ready to be undone.

I had circled this city for years in my imagination. Every pilgrim tradition I had followed — from the Pashupatinath temple steps in Kathmandu to the wind-scoured plains of Darchen at the foot of Kailash — eventually pointed here, to Kashi, the city that is said to be older than history and indifferent to it. People come to Varanasi to bathe, to pray, to die, and occasionally to simply sit with the fact that all three are the same gesture. I came by boat, which felt right. Arriving on water means the city reveals itself slowly, as a series of walls and spires and stone terraces climbing away from the river — not all at once, but in layers, like something being remembered rather than seen for the first time.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

The Ganges here is wide, brownish, unhurried. From a wooden rowing boat — the kind that creaks and rocks with each stroke of the oar — the ghats appear as a continuous stone cliff-face punctuated by temple shikhara towers, their sandstone spires pressing into a pale, overcast sky. The light is diffused, flat, almost winter-grey, and it does something unusual to the buildings: strips away the postcard quality and makes them simply old. Genuinely, heavily old.

The smell reaches you before the details do. Woodsmoke, river water, incense, marigold, and beneath all of it something that is not quite any of those things — the particular atmosphere of a place where cremation fires have burned without pause for centuries. From the left side of the riverbank, smoke rises in slow columns from Manikarnika Ghat. It is not dramatic. It is steady. The way a hearth fire is steady.

The stone steps of the ghats descend in broad terraces to the waterline, worn smooth at their edges, darkened with river water and ritual. Standing on them — as I did before boarding the boat — the ground is uneven underfoot, slightly damp, alive with pigeons and the distant percussion of temple bells. The air sits heavy and cool against the skin. The water laps at the lowest steps with a sound that is almost gentle, which surprises you every time.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

There is a particular thought that comes on the river. It arrives not as idea but as sensation — a loosening of the boundary between the one watching and the thing being watched.

The ghats do not perform. The smoke does not perform. The river certainly does not. And so the witness in me — the one who came here with a phone and intentions and a sense of being a self making a pilgrimage — begins to feel slightly beside the point. Advaita Vedanta teaches that the witness and the witnessed are not two things. Varanasi does not teach this. It simply makes the concept feel less abstract by surrounding you with its most uncompromising illustration: life and death occupying the same stone steps, the same river, the same morning.

I thought of what I had written after standing at the foot of Potala Palace in Lhasa — that sense of architecture built not to impress but to orient. Varanasi does something similar, but rawer. There is no grandeur here designed to elevate the spirit. There is only the river, the fire, the stone, and the question of who exactly is standing between them.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** न जायते म्रियते वा कदाचिन् नायं भूत्वा भविता वा न भूयः। अजो नित्यः शाश्वतोऽयं पुराणो न हन्यते हन्यमाने शरीरे॥

**Transliteration:** Na jāyate mriyate vā kadācin nāyaṃ bhūtvā bhavitā vā na bhūyaḥ। Ajo nityaḥ śāśvato'yaṃ purāṇo na hanyate hanyamāne śarīre॥

**Meaning:** The soul is never born nor dies at any time. It has not come into being, does not come into being, and will not come into being. It is unborn, eternal, ever-existing, and primeval. It is not slain when the body is slain.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2, Verse 20

**Relevance:** On the river at Manikarnika Ghat, with smoke rising from pyres that have not been extinguished in living memory, this verse stops being theology and becomes something closer to direct observation. The body returns to its elements here, visibly, without ceremony designed to soften the fact. What remains — what the verse points to — becomes the only honest question worth sitting with.

## Practical Travel Notes

Varanasi is accessible by air (Lal Bahadur Shastri Airport, about 25 km from the ghats) and by train — the rail network connects it to most major Indian cities. No special permits are required.

The best seasons are October through March, when the heat is manageable and the river is at a navigable level. Summers are brutal — 40°C-plus with high humidity. The monsoon months bring flooding that submerges the lower ghat steps entirely.

For the boat ride, hire a wooden rowboat rather than a motorboat — the noise of an engine cuts through the atmosphere considerably. Early morning is the most rewarding time on the water: the aarti fires from the previous night have settled, and the light, even when overcast, has a particular quality that midday erases.

Wear sturdy sandals or shoes you do not mind getting wet or soiled on the lower steps. The ghats are uneven and occasionally slippery. There is no altitude concern here, but the sensory density of the place is its own kind of intensity — give yourself unhurried time.

Stay in the old city (the lanes behind the ghats) if you want proximity to the river rhythm. It is genuinely narrow, noisy, and alive at all hours.

## Closing Reflection

The boat returned to the ghat as the overcast sky began to thin slightly at its edges — not quite sunlight, but a brightening. The temple spires held their positions above the roofline with the patience of things that have outlasted every argument about their meaning.

I did not leave Varanasi feeling resolved. That is not what the place offers. It offers, instead, a very clear look at what you brought with you — every assumption about permanence, about the self, about what constitutes an arrival. The river keeps moving. The steps stay. The smoke rises and disperses. None of it waits for you to finish thinking about it.
