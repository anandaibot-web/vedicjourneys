## Introduction – Why This Journey

The sun had just begun to warm the cool morning air as I ascended the steps of the grand monastery nestled on a hill in Chengguan District, Tibet. The date was July 8, 2025, and my journey had brought me to an altitude of 3617.9 meters, where the world felt both vast and intimate. Drawn by the allure of ancient tradition and the promise of serene reflection, I found myself amidst monks clad in vibrant maroon robes, their ritual instruments resonating through the clear, crisp air.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

The monastery stood proud against a backdrop of a flawless azure sky. Architecturally magnificent, its white walls glistened under the sunlight, creating a striking contrast against the lush greenery of the hillside. The physicality of the journey, at such great altitude, demanded a strong heart and steady footing, for every breath came with its own effort, echoing the silent rhythm of the mountain breeze.

As I observed the monks conducting their religious ceremonies, an overwhelming sense of peace enveloped me. The ceremonial drums thumped while long horns reverberated across the courtyard, harmonizing with the sound of temple bells, creating a symphony that transcended time. The air carried a subtle chill, hinting at the fiery warmth of the sun sheltered by the heights.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

In this sanctuary, where the line between the external world and inner consciousness blurs, I became a witness to my own thoughts. Advaita, the philosophy of non-duality, seemed palpable. In the complex dance of sound, color, and tradition, I felt a transformation—not towards something new, but a deepened connection with what always was. Here, dualities dissolved into oneness; the observer and the observed united seamlessly.

## A Sloka for the Path

In this serene abode, the sloka from the Bhagavad Gita found my silent attention:

"योगस्थः कुरु कर्माणि सङ्गं त्यक्त्वा धनञ्जय |
सिद्ध्यसिद्ध्योः समो भूत्वा समत्वं योग उच्यते ||"

*Yogasthaḥ kuru karmāṇi saṅgaṁ tyaktvā dhanañjaya |
Siddhyasiddhyoḥ samo bhūtvā samatvaṁ yoga ucyate ||* (Bhagavad Gita 2.48)

Meaning: "Perform your duties established in Yoga, O Arjuna, abandoning attachment and remaining even-minded in success and failure. Such equanimity is called Yoga."

The relevance of this verse in this tranquil setting was profound. It served as a reminder that the true essence of yoga is equanimity amidst the oscillations of existence.

Ensure that physical preparations are made for high altitude, allowing the body time to acclimatize. Traditional events are common, an opportunity embraced best with respect and mindfulness. ## Closing Reflection

As my journey drew to a close, I glanced back at the monastery perched gracefully atop the hill—a reflection of resilience and tranquility. Understanding had not arrived like a sudden epiphany; rather, it unfolded quietly, much like the petals opening to the morning sun. Here, in Tibet’s heart, serenity welcomed me, forever etching its gentle wisdom into the canvas of my soul.
