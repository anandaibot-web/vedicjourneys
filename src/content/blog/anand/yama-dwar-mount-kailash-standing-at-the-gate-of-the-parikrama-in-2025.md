## Introduction – Why This Journey

The banner says it plainly: *Mount Kailash & Mansarovar Yatra 2025 — Shiva Parvati Parivara Puja | 24 June – 12 July*. There is nothing ambiguous about why forty-odd people are standing here at Yama Dwar on the morning of 2 July 2025, waving at a camera in front of a white-painted Buddhist stupa banded in red, blue, and yellow, its golden spire catching whatever thin light leaks through the cumulus clouds overhead. The reason is written on the banner, stitched into the itinerary, and — if you are honest — written somewhere older than either.

Yama Dwar is the ceremonial gateway to the Kailash parikrama circuit. In Hindu tradition, it is the threshold you cross to begin circumambulating the mountain — the point at which ordinary travel ends and something more deliberate begins. I did not arrive here chasing a feeling. I arrived because the journey from Darchen to this gate is a physical commitment made on foot, and the body, at this altitude, becomes the most honest record-keeper you have.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

The terrain at Yama Dwar is sparse in the way that only high Tibetan plateau can be — tufts of pale grass pushing through rocky debris, the ground neither welcoming nor hostile, simply indifferent. Arid ridgelines rise steeply on both sides, their exposed rock faces layered in the rust and brown that marks this corner of the Kailash Mansarovar region. There is no water visible here — no lake, no stream cutting across the plateau floor — just open land running back toward a small white structure faintly visible in the far right of the scene.

The sky is doing its characteristic high-altitude performance: large cumulus clouds stacking against patches of actual blue, overcast in one direction and open in another. In photographs this looks dramatic. In person, it means you keep your down jacket zipped and your gloves on, because the wind at this elevation does not negotiate. Almost everyone in the group is dressed accordingly — insulated jackets in yellow, blue, orange, and grey; woolly hats pulled low; scarves up around the face; a few wearing surgical masks against the dust and cold. The cold here is not the soft cold of a hill station morning. It is dry, thin, and patient.

Colorful prayer flags are strung in lines across the background, snapping whenever the wind moves through. To the right of the stupa, mani stones carry Tibetan script carved carefully into their surfaces — someone's hours of work left permanently at the gate. The stupa itself stands clean and white against the mountain ridge, its tiered base and banded mid-section grounding the eye before the golden spire draws it upward. It is a well-maintained structure, its geometry precise, its presence matter-of-fact.

Physically, the effort of being here is real. The altitude at Yama Dwar sits well above 4,500 metres. Each breath pulls in less than your body expects. You move more slowly than you think you need to. The group photograph — forty-plus people pressed together, waving, some grinning behind masks — captures a moment of collective relief as much as celebration. You made it to the gate. That is genuinely worth marking.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

There is a particular quality of attention that arrives uninvited at high altitude. The mind, stripped of the usual background hum of comfort and option, becomes quieter. Not peaceful in the greeting-card sense — just less cluttered. Standing at Yama Dwar, looking at forty people who have each carried their own reasons across several thousand kilometres of logistics and border crossings and altitude sickness, something becomes clear.

The gate is not separating the sacred from the ordinary. It is simply a marker where you agree to pay attention differently. The mountain does not change when you cross it. You do not change. But the agreement shifts — you are here now, walking in a circle around something that does not require your interpretation to be what it is.

Advaita Vedanta would call this the recognition of the witness — the part of awareness that watches the pilgrim walk, watches the cold bite, watches the banner flutter, without being produced by any of those things. The group photograph is taken at 04:13 on a July morning in 2025. The camera records that fact. Something else records the fact of the recording. That second register — impersonal, steady, without preference — is what the parikrama, done honestly, tends to expose. You do not find it. You stop covering it with noise.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:**
> यदा भूतपृथग्भावमेकस्थमनुपश्यति ।
> तत एव च विस्तारं ब्रह्म सम्पद्यते तदा ॥

**Transliteration:**
*Yadā bhūta-pṛthag-bhāvam eka-stham anupaśyati,
tata eva ca vistāraṃ brahma sampadyate tadā.*

**Meaning:** When one perceives the separate existence of all beings as resting in the One, and their expansion from that One alone — one then attains Brahman.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 13, Verse 30

**Relevance:** At Yama Dwar, forty people from different backgrounds stand at the same gate, wearing the same cold-weather gear, breathing the same thin air, carrying the same banner. The diversity is obvious. What the verse points to is the quieter fact underneath it — that the awareness meeting this scene is not plural. The stupa, the mani stones, the prayer flags, the ridgeline, the clouds — none of it is happening to separate experiencers. It is appearing within one field. The parikrama is, among other things, a long walk around that recognition.

## Practical Travel Notes

The Mount Kailash & Mansarovar Yatra 2025 group pictured here traveled between 24 June and 12 July. The photograph at Yama Dwar was taken on 2 July 2025. Yama Dwar marks the formal start of the Kailash parikrama and sits on the plateau approaching the mountain from Darchen.

Essential preparation notes for this section of the route: Cold-weather layering is non-negotiable regardless of summer dates — down jackets, thermal base layers, windproof outer shells, gloves, and warm headwear are all visible in this group and all necessary. Altitude acclimatization before beginning the parikrama is critical; most groups spend time at Darchen before proceeding to the gate. Faces masks are useful — dust and dry air are persistent at this elevation. The terrain is rocky and uneven; sturdy footwear with ankle support matters. The altitude here exceeds 4,500 metres and GPS coordinates for this location were not recorded at time of capture.

Group travel through this region requires permits arranged well in advance through authorized tour operators. The banner explicitly identifies the event as a *Shiva Parvati Parivara Puja* yatra, suggesting the group combines pilgrimage intention with organized tour logistics — a practical approach for this region given permit requirements and logistical complexity.

## Closing Reflection

The photograph exists now — forty-plus people, one banner, one stupa, one sky doing its complicated thing above the Tibetan plateau. Taken at 04:13 on 2 July 2025, it is a record of a specific morning at a specific gate. What it cannot record is the quality of air, the particular resistance of legs at altitude, or the way the prayer flags sounded in the wind before anyone called out to look at the camera.

Yama Dwar does not ask you to feel anything particular. It simply stands at the edge of the circuit and notes your arrival. Whether you cross it once or never again, the gate remains. The mountain remains. The walk, if you take it, will be yours to account for — step by step, breath by breath, one revolution around something that holds its shape regardless of who is watching.
