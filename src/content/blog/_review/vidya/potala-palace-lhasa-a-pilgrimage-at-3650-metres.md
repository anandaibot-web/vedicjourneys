## Why This Place Pulls You Before You Understand It

I did not come to Lhasa because I had planned it carefully. I came because something in me had been leaning toward it for years — the way a plant leans without knowing it is leaning toward light. There are places on earth that do not wait for you to be ready. Potala Palace is one of them. It sits on Marpori Hill above the valley floor as though it has always existed and always will — massive, composed, indifferent to human urgency. Standing at its base for the first time, I felt less like a visitor and more like something that had finally arrived where it was supposed to be. That feeling, I would learn, is the beginning of the undoing this place quietly arranges.

## The Landscape and What the Body Learns Here

The air at 3,649 metres is not thin in the way the phrase suggests. It is complete air — just less of it than the lungs have been trained to expect. Every step up the stone staircase costs something. I noticed this immediately: the slight drag in the chest, the pause my body inserted between effort and recovery. The sun at this altitude is not warm so much as direct — it hits the whitewashed walls and bounces back as pure light, the kind that makes you squint even with your eyes half-closed. The sky above Lhasa in July is a blue so saturated it looks painted. No cloud. No softening. Just that hard, clean vault overhead.

The walls of the palace rise in massive sloping tiers, the lower sections whitewashed and thick as a fortress, the upper storeys deep maroon, topped with gold finial spires that catch the noon sun and hold it. Auspicious symbols — the endless knot, the dharma wheel — are worked into the dark facade panels in a way that feels neither decorative nor incidental. They are statements. The stone steps underfoot are worn smooth by centuries of feet, and I felt that smoothness through my soles like a kind of quiet testimony.

To the left, a yellow ochre structure with a covered wooden colonnade anchors the courtyard, its gallery casting the only shade available. I rested there briefly, hand on the wall, breathing slowly as I had read to do — short breaths, no hurry, let the body find its own negotiation with the altitude. Fellow pilgrims moved up and down the stairs in steady rhythm, no one rushing. The mountain does not reward rushing.

The sound is what I remember most when I close my eyes now. Wind, occasional and low. The scrape of footsteps on stone. A kind of resonant quiet underneath everything — the silence that very high places carry inside them.

## What the Altitude Does to the Sense of Self

There is something physiologically honest about altitude. It strips you of the performance of ease. You cannot pretend the climb is effortless when your lungs are making their limits audible. And in that honesty, the identity you carry — the story of who you are, what you've achieved, what you're going to do next — becomes strangely irrelevant. The body is too occupied with the immediate fact of breathing.

I have written before, in my account of [Kedarnath](/journey-to-kedarnath-reflections-by-the-turquoise-lake), about how high places have a way of doing what meditation only sometimes manages: they make the witness apparent. Standing against the white wall of Potala, pausing between breaths, watching my own thoughts arrive and pass like the small gusts of wind across the courtyard — I noticed the one who was watching. Not dramatically. Not in the way epiphanies are described. Just quietly, plainly: the awareness that was present before I climbed these stairs will be present after I descend them. The stairs, the effort, the golden spires, the blue sky — all of it arising in something that remains still.

Advaita does not ask you to feel this. It simply points to what is already the case. The palace has been pointing at something similar for three centuries.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** नित्यः सर्वगतः स्थाणुरचलोऽयं सनातनः

**Transliteration:** Nityaḥ sarva-gataḥ sthāṇur acalo'yaṁ sanātanaḥ

**Meaning:** This Self is eternal, all-pervading, unchanging, immovable, and primordial.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2, Verse 24

**Relevance:** On a staircase where each step costs breath and the body reminds you of its limits, the verse does not offer comfort so much as orientation. What labours up these stairs is not what you fundamentally are. The gold on the roofline catches light and releases it; what witnesses that catching and releasing is neither gained nor lost by the journey.

## Practical Travel Notes

Entry to Tibet requires a Tibet Travel Permit in addition to a Chinese visa — these cannot be obtained independently; you must book through a registered Tibetan travel agency, which will arrange both permits and a mandatory guide. Allow at least two to three days in Lhasa before attempting Potala Palace to acclimatise. Altitude sickness is real and not predictable by fitness level; ascend slowly, hydrate consistently, and carry acetazolamide only if prescribed.

July is high season — clear skies, warm days, but expect some afternoon thunderstorm activity in the broader region. Entry to Potala is ticketed and strictly limited daily; book well in advance through official channels. The visit is timed, so know your window.

For gear: sun protection is non-negotiable at this elevation — SPF 50 minimum, quality sunglasses. A light wind layer for the exposed staircases and courtyard. Comfortable, broken-in walking shoes with grip. Carry water but drink steadily rather than in large amounts.

Lhasa itself rewards slow mornings. The Barkhor circuit around Jokhang Temple is best walked at dawn, before the crowds and before the sun becomes insistent.

## Closing

I descended the stone stairs in the mid-afternoon, the gold finials behind me still catching the high sun. My legs were steady by then. My breathing had found its pace. Nothing had happened in the way that things are supposed to happen at famous places. And yet something had been quietly rearranged — not in me exactly, but in the distance I keep between myself and what I experience. Potala does not give you anything. It simply makes certain habits of distance harder to maintain. That seems like enough.
