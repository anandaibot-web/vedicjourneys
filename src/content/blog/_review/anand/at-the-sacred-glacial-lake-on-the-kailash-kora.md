## Introduction – Why This Journey

There is no rational explanation for why a person crosses scree fields at altitude, lungs burning, legs uncertain beneath them, to reach a small body of water cradled in rock. The lake does not need you there. The mountain has no opinion on your arrival. And yet something in the human animal — or perhaps something older than the human animal — insists on making the approach. Insists on standing at the edge.

I had read about this place long before I ever stood anywhere near it. I had understood, intellectually, that the kora around Kailash contained within it smaller acts of devotion — subsidiary lakes, subsidiary surrenders. But understanding a thing from a distance is a very different event from placing your boot on loose moraine and feeling the stone shift beneath your weight as the air offers your lungs roughly half of what they expect. I came here because I had come to Kailash. And I came to Kailash because something in me needed to be reduced. Not broken. Reduced. There is a difference.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

The descent to the lake is not gentle. The hillside is entirely scree — loose, angular, indifferent — and each step requires a small negotiation between intention and gravity. The rocks are the grey-brown of old bone, piled in the manner that only glacial retreat produces: no order, no hierarchy, just accumulated evidence of a slow withdrawal over centuries.

The lake itself arrives suddenly. You come around a boulder or crest a small rise and there it is — that colour. That impossible, luminous turquoise, the milky blue-green that only glacial silt suspension produces, sitting in its moraine bowl with the casual authority of something that has been there long before the prayer flags arrived. The sheer cliff faces of the flanking peaks rise on either side, dark and vertical, closing the valley into something that feels more like a chamber than a landscape. The sky above — partly open, partly veiled in that particular high-altitude haze — admits light at an angle that makes the water glow from within.

The cold is specific here. Not the ambient cold of an overcast day, but the cold that lives in the shadow of north-facing rock faces that see perhaps four hours of direct sun. It moves through clothing into the chest. Breathing at this altitude is already a conscious act — each inhalation a deliberate effort, each exhalation a small letting go.

The prayer flags are everywhere in the foreground rocks — dense, layered, weathered to varying degrees of fading. Some are vivid still, strung recently, snapping when the valley draws a breath of wind. Others are almost transparent with age, their lungta wind-horse prints barely visible, the cloth returning slowly to the elements that produced it. Offering items are tucked into the crevices between boulders. Someone has placed something here and walked away. That gesture — the placing and the walking away — is the entire grammar of this site.

Fellow pilgrims move near the water's edge quietly. There is very little conversation at altitude. The body has other priorities.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

Standing above that lake, looking down at water that colour, flanked by mountains that size, with this little oxygen available for thought — the usual interior monologue simply cannot sustain itself. It runs out of material. The self that narrates, that compares and evaluates and plans, finds nothing here to grip.

In Advaita terms, what remains when the narrator goes quiet is not emptiness. It is the prior condition — awareness itself, without content. The lake does not know it is beautiful. The mountain does not know it is vast. The flags do not know they carry prayers. And in that moment, standing on scree with burning legs and a quieted mind, I found I did not quite know who was knowing any of it.

This is not a dramatic dissolution. It is more like the moment between exhale and inhale — brief, unclaimable, and strangely familiar. As though this were the ground state, and everything else — the planning, the striving, the journey itself — were the elaboration on top of it. The kora had brought me here not to find something, but to stop adding.

I had felt something adjacent to this at Yama Dwar, at the very gate of Kailash — that sense of threshold, of being asked to leave something behind before proceeding. This lake felt like a confirmation of what that gate had promised.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** यदा ते मोहकलिलं बुद्धिर्व्यतितरिष्यति। तदा गन्तासि निर्वेदं श्रोतव्यस्य श्रुतस्य च।।

**Transliteration:** Yadā te moha-kalilaṁ buddhir vyatitariṣyati | tadā gantāsi nirvedaṁ śrotavyasya śrutasya ca ||

**Meaning:** When your intellect crosses beyond the thicket of delusion, you will become indifferent to all that has been heard and all that is yet to be heard.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2, Verse 52

**Relevance:** At this altitude, beside this water, the accumulated architecture of what I thought I knew — about pilgrimage, about devotion, about what arrival should feel like — became very quiet. This verse does not promise peace through acquisition but through the falling away of the need to keep acquiring understanding. That falling away happened here, unbidden, between one breath and the next.

## Practical Travel Notes

This lake is accessible as part of the inner kora of Mount Kailash, typically reached on the second day of the three-day circuit. The approach involves significant altitude gain, with the surrounding terrain sitting well above 5,000 metres. Altitude acclimatisation is not optional here — spend at least two nights in Darchen and ideally one in Dirmapuk before attempting this section.

Permits required include the Tibet Travel Permit, Alien's Travel Permit, and the Kailash restricted area permit — all arranged through a registered Tibetan travel agency; independent travel is not permitted. The kora season runs from May through October, with June and September generally offering the most stable weather. March conditions — as on this visit — mean colder temperatures, possible snow on the high pass, and fewer other pilgrims on the route.

Gear essentials: trekking poles are non-negotiable on scree, a reliable sleeping bag rated to -15°C, high-altitude sun protection, and water purification. Acclimatisation medication is worth discussing with a physician before departure. Pack light but pack warm layers — the temperature differential between midday sun and any shade is dramatic at this elevation.

## Closing Reflection

The lake holds its colour regardless of whether anyone is watching. The flags continue their slow unravelling whether or not the prayers printed on them are ever read. The mountain stands.

I did not arrive here and become different. I arrived here and became, briefly, less insistent on being anything in particular. That is a subtler thing, and probably more durable. The scree did not care about my difficulty descending it. The water did not shimmer for my benefit. Both of those facts were, unexpectedly, a relief.
