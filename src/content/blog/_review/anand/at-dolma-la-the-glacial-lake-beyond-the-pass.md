## Introduction – Why This Journey

There is no rational explanation for why a person crosses a Himalayan pass at over 5,600 metres. The body resists it. The lungs protest at every step. And yet something older than reason keeps pulling — a direction more than a destination, a question the feet ask before the mind frames it.

I had read about Dolma La and the lake that sits just below its crest the way one reads about things that feel both impossible and inevitable. The Kora around Mount Kailash had already begun to strip something away from me — I had written about the first steps out of Darchen and the crossing toward Dalmala Pass in an earlier entry, and by the time I reached this point in the circuit, I understood that this was not a trek. It was an unravelling, done slowly, at altitude, one laboured breath at a time.

The lake appeared before I was ready for it.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

I smelled the cold before I saw the water. At this elevation, the air has a particular quality — thin, almost metallic, carrying nothing organic, no soil, no bark, no decay. Just stone and sky and the faint mineral trace of glacial melt.

The terrain underfoot is all scree — loose, angular, unforgiving. Each step requires a small negotiation: plant, test, shift weight, commit. The slope does not forgive distraction. My thighs ached with a deep bone-tiredness that is distinct from ordinary fatigue. This is altitude fatigue, where the muscles are not simply tired but subtly starved.

And then the lake came into view below — a basin of impossible turquoise cradled entirely in grey rock. No vegetation. No gentleness in the surrounding landscape. Just those steep, jagged flanks rising on either side, dark stone spires catching the morning light at the ridge, and in the middle of all that austerity, this eye of water, completely still, holding a colour that seemed borrowed from somewhere warmer and kinder.

Prayer flags covered the rocks in the foreground — dense, layered, some new and taut in the wind, others so weathered they had softened into the stone beneath them. They cracked and snapped irregularly. That sound — not musical, more like the punctuation of intention — was the only sound besides my own breathing. Small offerings had been pressed into the crevices around them: coins, locks of hair, items I couldn't identify, left by people who had stood exactly here, feeling exactly this.

The sky was partly open, partly cloud. When the sun broke through, the lake shifted shade — from pale jade to something almost luminous. When cloud passed over, it darkened to grey-green, cold and interior. I stood watching it for a long time without consciously choosing to stop.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

There is a peculiar trick altitude plays on the thinking mind. At sea level, thoughts arrive in full sentences, complete and sequential. Up here, they come in fragments, then stop. The mind runs out of momentum the way the breath does.

What remained, standing above that lake, was not thought. It was something simpler — awareness without commentary. The lake was there. The stone was there. The wind moved the flags. I was there, watching, but the watcher felt increasingly difficult to locate precisely. Not absent — just without fixed address.

This is what Advaita points to, I think, not the dramatic dissolution of self but this quiet difficulty in finding where the observer ends and the observed begins. The turquoise lake did not need me to witness it. The mountains were entirely indifferent to my presence. And in that indifference, paradoxically, was something close to rest — the rest of not being required to be someone in particular.

The offering items tucked among the flags — each one left by a person who stood here, wanted something, believed in the wanting — reminded me that devotion and philosophy are not opposites. Both are attempts to put down a burden. The form differs. The relief, perhaps, is the same.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** यं हि न व्यथयन्त्येते पुरुषं पुरुषर्षभ। समदुःखसुखं धीरं सोऽमृतत्वाय कल्पते।।

**Transliteration:** Yaṃ hi na vyathayantyete puruṣaṃ puruṣarṣabha, sama-duḥkha-sukhaṃ dhīraṃ so'mṛtatvāya kalpate.

**Meaning:** That person whom these (pairs of opposites — heat and cold, pleasure and pain) do not disturb, who is steady and equal in both suffering and joy — that one is fit for immortality.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2, Verse 15

**Relevance:** Standing between the biting cold of the pass and the impossible beauty of the lake below, the body is pulled between effort and wonder, discomfort and awe. This verse does not ask us to deny either — it asks for steadiness inside both. That steadiness, I found, is not something one brings to Dolma La. It is something the mountain slowly extracts.

## Practical Travel Notes

Dolma La Pass sits at approximately 5,636 metres and is the highest point of the Kailash Kora. The glacial lake just below — Gauri Kund — is considered sacred in both Hindu and Buddhist traditions, though access to the water's edge requires careful footing on loose rock.

**Permits:** Foreign nationals require a Tibet Travel Permit, Alien's Travel Permit, and a Military Permit. These are arranged through a registered Chinese travel agency and cannot be obtained independently. Indian nationals travelling via the Mansarovar Yatra route require coordination through official government channels.

**Best season:** Late May through early September. The Kora is typically open from May, with peak conditions in June and July. March travel, as this trip suggests, is early-season — cold is significant, snowpack on the pass may be present, and altitude acclimatisation is critical.

**Acclimatisation:** Do not rush to Darchen. Spend at least two to three nights in Lhasa, then additional time at Shigatse or Saga. Ascend gradually. Altitude sickness at Dolma La is not a risk to minimise — it is a variable to plan around.

**Gear essentials:** Layered insulation, waterproof outer shell, trekking poles (non-negotiable on scree descent), high-altitude sleeping bag, and blister management. The descent from Dolma La is steep and technically demanding on tired legs.

**Logistics:** Most pilgrims complete the 52-kilometre Kora in three days, staying at tea houses in Dirapuk and Zuthulpuk. Ponies and yaks are available for gear support. Day two, which includes Dolma La, is the hardest and longest.

## Closing Reflection

I descended from the pass slowly, the lake dropping out of sight behind a fold of rock. I kept turning back, not from sentiment but from disbelief — that something that colour could exist in a place that bare.

What I carried down was not certainty or peace in any comfortable sense. It was something quieter: the memory of standing somewhere that asked nothing of me, where the mountain was simply the mountain and the water was simply the water, and whatever I thought I was had briefly, mercifully, no particular claim on the moment.

That is enough. That is more than enough.
