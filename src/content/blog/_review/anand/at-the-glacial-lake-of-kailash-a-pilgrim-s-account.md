## Introduction – Why This Journey

There is no rational explanation for why a person crosses half the world to stand on a scree slope at extreme altitude, lungs half-empty, legs aching, eyes watering from cold wind and sharp sunlight. The explanation lives somewhere below language — in the pull that began long before the permits were filed, long before the boots were broken in. The Kailash kora had been sitting at the edge of my awareness for years, the way certain debts do. Not urgent. Just present. Quietly waiting.

I had written about standing at Yama Dwar — the gate of Kailash — and felt even then that the mountain was not something you approached so much as something that received you, or didn't. This lake, tucked into a rocky basin high on the circuit, felt like a different kind of threshold. Not a gate but a mirror. Still, turquoise, impossibly clear in its color against all that grey stone.

I came here because I needed to see something that did not care whether I arrived.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

The scree underfoot is the first thing the body learns. Each step shifts slightly — small flat stones riding larger ones, a constant negotiation between weight and balance. There is no path here that stays put. The mountain reshapes the ground constantly, indifferently.

The air is thin in a way that words approximate poorly. It is not that breathing is painful — it is that each breath feels partial, like receiving half a meal. My pace had slowed to something I wouldn't have called walking back home. More like a considered placing of one foot, then a pause, then the other. The sunlight is fierce and flat at this altitude. No humidity to soften it. It lands on the rock faces and bounces back doubled, and the shadows in the cliff spires to the left are so dark they look carved from a different material than the lit stone beside them.

The lake appears suddenly. You are picking your way down through boulders and then it is simply there — an oval of impossible turquoise held in grey rock like something placed rather than formed. The color comes from glacial rock flour suspended in the melt, I know this intellectually, but the knowing does nothing to reduce the effect. It looks lit from within. The surrounding peaks — jagged ridgelines rising on both flanks — frame it without softening it. There is no vegetation. Not a blade of grass. Only rock, sky, water, and wind.

The prayer flags begin before you reach the lakeshore. They are strung in every direction across the foreground boulders, lungta in red, blue, yellow, green, white, orange — dense layers of them, wind-frayed and faded at the edges, newer ones bright on top. Beneath them, tucked among the rocks, offerings: small things left by hands that were here before mine. The flags snap and shudder in the wind. It is the only constant sound. That, and my own breathing.

Fellow pilgrims move quietly near the water. Nobody is talking loudly. The place does not invite noise.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

Standing at the edge of that water, I noticed something that I had been circling around for days on the kora. The self that had planned this trip, packed bags, arranged permits — that self had been steadily dismantled by altitude and physical effort. What remained was simpler. A body breathing. Eyes receiving light. Feet registering cold stone.

Advaita Vedanta speaks of the witness — *sakshi* — the awareness that observes experience without being consumed by it. On a comfortable cushion in a warm room, that teaching feels available, even pleasant. Here it was not a teaching. It was simply what was left after everything effortful had been stripped away. The lake did not confirm my presence. The mountain did not acknowledge the journey. And in that refusal, something clarified.

The turquoise water held the sky. The sky held the peaks. The peaks held nothing — they simply were. And for a few minutes, standing there with the flags rattling and my breath short and the cold working into my fingers, I was not separate from any of it in the way I usually assume I am.

That is all. Not revelation. Just a brief, honest loosening.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** यदा भूतपृथग्भावमेकस्थमनुपश्यति। तत एव च विस्तारं ब्रह्म सम्पद्यते तदा॥

**Transliteration:** Yadā bhūtapṛthagbhāvam ekastham anupaśyati / tata eva ca vistāraṃ brahma sampadyate tadā

**Meaning:** When one perceives the diversity of all beings as resting in the One, and the expansion of all from that One alone — then one attains Brahman.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 13, Verse 30

**Relevance:** At this lake, surrounded by bare rock and sky and wind and that strange, still water, the separateness I carry daily became briefly unconvincing. The peaks, the flags, the cold, the other pilgrims moving silently — all of it rested in the same field of awareness. This verse does not describe an achievement. It describes a recognition, and this place has a way of making that recognition available whether one seeks it or not.

## Practical Travel Notes

This lake sits within the Kailash kora circuit in the Tibet Autonomous Region. Access requires a China Group Visa, a Tibet Travel Permit, and an Alien's Travel Permit — none of which can be obtained independently. All foreign visitors must travel with a registered tour operator and licensed guide. Plan for this paperwork to take several weeks minimum.

The kora itself is approximately 52 kilometres. Most pilgrims complete it in three days, though two days is possible for the well-acclimatised. The highest point on the circuit is Drolma La pass at roughly 5,630 metres. This lake lies near that section of the route.

Acclimatisation is not optional. Spend at minimum two nights at Darchen (approximately 4,575m) before beginning. Many pilgrims spend a night in Lhasa and another at a mid-altitude stop. Acute Mountain Sickness is a genuine risk. Carry diamox if your doctor approves, but treat it as a supplement to acclimatisation, not a substitute.

Gear priorities: layering for extreme temperature swings, trekking poles for scree descent, a reliable sleeping bag rated to -15°C minimum, and broken-in waterproof boots with ankle support. Teahouses exist along the route but are basic — carry your own snacks and a water purification method.

March is early season. Expect cold nights, possible high winds, and the chance of clear skies that make the light extraordinary.

## Closing Reflection

I left a small stone near the prayer flags. Not ceremonially. I just set it down among the others that had been set down before mine, by people whose names I will never know, for reasons I can only guess at.

The lake stayed turquoise. The wind kept moving the flags. The mountain did not mark my visit in any way.

There is something genuinely settling about a place that holds no record of your having been there. You carry the experience back down with you, through the scree and the thin air and the long road home. The lake stays exactly as it was. That is, I think, the point.
