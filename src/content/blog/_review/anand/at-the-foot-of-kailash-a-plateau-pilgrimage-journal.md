## Introduction – Why This Journey

There is no rational explanation for why a person crosses three countries, ascends beyond five thousand metres, and walks on a barren plateau where the wind does not so much blow as insist. The pull of Kailash is not geographic. It predates maps. I had read about it for years — in the Shiva Purana, in the quiet remarks of people who had been there and returned somehow quieter — and I understood intellectually that this mountain is considered the axis of the world, the seat of Shiva, the still point around which everything rotates. But understanding and arriving are two entirely different acts. On the morning we reached the plateau valley near Darchen, I stepped out of the vehicle and the cold entered my lungs like a correction. The body immediately knew: this is not ordinary ground.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

The plateau opens without warning. One moment there are switchbacks and loose scree dropping away on both sides, and then suddenly — flatness. A broad, wind-scoured valley ringed by layered sedimentary ridges the colour of old rust and dry bone. The rock faces read like geological scripture, each stratum a different era pressed into visibility.

The stupa stands near the valley floor, white and deliberate, its red dome catching the grey morning light. It does not ask for attention. It simply occupies the space with the quiet authority of something that has outlasted several generations of weather. Prayer flags run in long horizontal lines behind it, each one faded to a pale version of its original colour — the wind has been reading them for years. Beside a low mani stone wall, Tibetan script carved deep into rock runs for metres; each syllable chiselled by hand, each hand moved by faith repeated until it became muscle memory.

At this altitude, the sky is not background. It is participant. Large cumulus clouds build and dissolve in the same hour, blue patches appearing and closing like eyes. The light shifts constantly — one minute flat and grey, the next, a shaft of cold gold crossing the plateau at a low angle. You cannot take it for granted. You cannot photograph it the way you imagine you will.

My breath is audible to me. Each inhale is a negotiation. The body burns more to do less — standing still costs something here. Fellow pilgrims around me move carefully, conserving, their footsteps deliberate on the sparse, frost-bitten grass. The cold is not hostile; it is simply indifferent, and that indifference is its own instruction.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

There is a particular quality of ego-softening that happens when the body is working this hard just to breathe. The internal monologue — the constant narrator who comments on everything — quiets down when oxygen is scarce. What remains is more immediate: the cold on the cheek, the sound of wind moving through prayer flags, the crunch of gravel underfoot.

Advaita Vedanta speaks of the witness — the awareness that observes experience without being consumed by it. On the plateau, that distinction becomes felt rather than philosophical. The 'I' who planned this trip, who worried about permits and altitude sickness, seems thin and provisional here. What remains is something that simply notices: the stupa, the flags, the ridgeline, the breath. Not mine. Not not-mine. Just present.

This is what I began to sense at Yama Dwar — that threshold feeling I wrote about in an earlier post — and it deepens here. The mountain does not acknowledge pilgrims. It does not reward arrival. That indifference is not cruelty; it is accuracy. The universe does not centre on the one who comes to see it.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** यदा भूतपृथग्भावमेकस्थमनुपश्यति। तत एव च विस्तारं ब्रह्म सम्पद्यते तदा।।

**Transliteration:** Yadā bhūta-pṛthag-bhāvam eka-stham anupaśyati / tata eva ca vistāraṃ brahma sampadyate tadā

**Meaning:** When one perceives the diversity of all beings as resting in the One, and their expansion as proceeding from that One alone — at that moment one attains Brahman.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 13, Verse 30

**Relevance:** Standing on a plateau where Buddhist, Hindu, and Bon traditions have converged for centuries around one mountain, the verse lands with unusual precision. The stupa, the mani stones, the prayer flags, the Sanskrit mantras carried by fellow pilgrims — different forms, one orientation. The mountain does not belong to any of them. It holds them all without preference.

## Practical Travel Notes

The Kailash-Mansarovar Yatra requires an Inner Line Permit for the Tibet Autonomous Region, coordinated through the Ministry of External Affairs (India) or through authorised operators for the Nepal–Tibet overland route. The journey typically runs June through September; late June through mid-July offers the most stable window before the heaviest monsoon disrupts road access.

Altitude is the primary challenge. Darchen sits above 4,500 metres; the Dolma La pass on the parikrama reaches over 5,600 metres. Acclimatise in Kathmandu or Lhasa for at least two to three days before pushing higher. Do not rush the ascent profile regardless of group pressure.

Essential gear: layered thermal base, windproof outer shell, trekking poles, and footwear rated for mixed terrain and sub-zero nights. Diamox is commonly used but consult a physician beforehand. Carry more water than you think you need — dehydration accelerates altitude symptoms. A small pulse oximeter is genuinely useful, not excessive.

Food at this altitude is simple: soups, rice, dal, tsampa. Eat what is offered. Your digestive system is already under stress.

## Closing Reflection

I stood near the stupa for longer than I intended. Not in meditation — just standing, watching the clouds reorganise themselves above the ridge. The flags moved. A stone rolled somewhere on the slope above. My breath steadied, finally, to something approaching normal.

Kailash does not give you an experience to carry home. It removes something — some layer of certainty about what is important — and leaves a quiet where that certainty used to sit. Whether that quiet is peace or simply exhaustion, I am still not sure. Both feel honest.
