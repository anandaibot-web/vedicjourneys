## Introduction – Why This Journey

There is a particular kind of pull that this place exerts — not loud, not dramatic. It is more like a quiet insistence. You hear about it from someone who has been, and something in the hearing already begins the journey. A god reclining on water. Asleep, or perhaps beyond sleep entirely — resting in that state where effort has dissolved and only pure being remains. That image lodged itself somewhere below my thinking mind, and months later I found myself standing at the edge of a sacred tank, marigold petals drifting past my feet, the stone cold and damp beneath my palms on the railing.

I had not come with a formal pilgrimage itinerary. I had come because I needed to see what it felt like to stand before something that refuses urgency.

## The Landscape & Physical Experience

The air here carries moisture and incense together — a heavy, sweet weight that coats the back of the throat. The sky above was sealed with flat grey cloud, the kind that diffuses light evenly and makes everything appear both close and timeless. No sharp shadows. No glare. Just a soft, even luminosity that seemed to rise from the water itself.

The sacred tank is dark green, almost opaque, with the settled stillness of water that has not moved in a long time. Petals — orange, red, pale pink — lay scattered across its surface, turning slowly in no particular direction. The sound here is layered: somewhere behind the stone columns, a bell rings and stops; a priest's low murmur continues beneath that; water drips from somewhere unseen. The stone underfoot is worn smooth by ten thousand barefoot approaches.

At the centre of the tank, emerging from the water like something dreamed rather than built, the great black stone figure of Vishnu reclines on the coiled body of Shesha, the cosmic serpent. The coils are massive, each curve thick as a tree trunk, worn dark and gleaming at the edges where hands have reached across the years. The marigold garlands draped across the deity are almost violent in their colour against that black stone — a saffron so saturated it seems to emit its own warmth in the grey morning light. Silver ornaments catch whatever diffuse light reaches them. The Sudarshana Chakra rests near the hand, still.

I stood there for a long time. The damp of the stone crept into my feet. I did not hurry.

## Inner Reflection – An Advaita Lens

What undoes you here is not awe, exactly. It is something quieter. Vishnu in the Anantashayana posture is not depicted mid-action. He is not striding, not lifting a mountain, not descending in protection. He is resting — the universe proceeding from and through him while he lies entirely still. Brahma is said to emerge from his navel. Creation unfolds. And the source of it simply reclines.

This is the image that Advaita keeps pointing toward: that the witness is not the doer. That awareness, the ground of everything, does not participate in the turbulence it holds. The dark water of the tank, the floating petals, the incense, my own standing there — all of it arising within something that does not move.

I had written, in an earlier reflection at Budhanilkantha, about feeling this same quality near water — the sense that the sacred tank is not incidental but structural, that water is the right element for this teaching. You cannot hold water. You can only float in it, or drown in it, or watch it carry everything past.

Here, I felt it again: the distinct sensation of being seen by something that has no eyes, known by something that makes no claim. Not comfort, exactly. More like relief at no longer needing to be the most important thing happening.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** नैनं छिन्दन्ति शस्त्राणि नैनं दहति पावकः। न चैनं क्लेदयन्त्यापो न शोषयति मारुतः॥

**Transliteration:** Nainaṃ chindanti śastrāṇi nainaṃ dahati pāvakaḥ / Na cainaṃ kledayantyāpo na śoṣayati mārutaḥ

**Meaning:** Weapons cannot cleave this; fire cannot burn this; water cannot wet this; wind cannot dry this.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 2, Verse 23

**Relevance:** Standing before a deity resting in water — untouched by it, held by it, yet entirely beyond it — this verse arrived with unusual precision. The sacred tank surrounds the reclining form, and yet what the form represents remains unaffected by the water, by time, by the moss and the wear of centuries. The verse is not about stone. It is about what the stone is pointing toward.

## Practical Travel Notes

This style of Jalashayana Vishnu shrine — with a sacred water tank surrounding the reclining deity — is found at several key sites across South Asia, including Budhanilkantha near Kathmandu and Padmanabhaswamy in Kerala. Research your specific destination carefully, as access rules differ significantly between them.

For temple tank sites in Nepal, no special trekking permits are required, but photography restrictions near the main deity are often strictly enforced — ask before raising a camera. For South Indian Vishnu temples, dress codes apply at the entrance: shoulders and legs covered, footwear removed well before the sanctum.

Best season for most water-tank temple visits in Nepal is October through March — the monsoon makes tank paths slippery and the water level unpredictable. In Kerala, the post-monsoon months of October through February offer the clearest skies and manageable heat.

Carry your own small offering if you wish to participate — marigolds are available from vendors outside most temple gates. Arrive early, before the morning puja concludes, if you want relative quiet at the tank's edge.

## Closing Reflection

I left as the grey light was beginning to thin slightly at the edges. The petals were still circling on the dark water. Nothing had resolved. No question had been answered. But something had loosened — the particular tightness that comes from believing your own motion matters more than your stillness. The god reclines. The water holds him. The marigolds drift. This has been happening every morning, with or without witnesses, for longer than I can usefully imagine. That fact, held honestly, is enough.
