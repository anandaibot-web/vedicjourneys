## Why This Journey

There are places you visit and places that summon you. Mount Kailash is the second kind. I had read about the parikrama — the 52-kilometre circumambulation of the sacred peak — for years before I actually came. It sits at the centre of four of the world's great religions, and yet what draws most people here, I suspect, has nothing to do with theology and everything to do with a nagging sense that certain questions can only be asked at altitude. I came because I needed to walk — or in this stretch of the kora, to ride — in a direction that felt earned.

The decision to take a horse for part of the circuit was practical before it was anything else. My legs had already learned respect for this plateau. The air here does not fill your lungs the way air is supposed to; it enters reluctantly, thinly, as though offering just enough to keep the argument going between you and the mountain.

## The Landscape and Physical Experience

The grassland on the western flank of the kora is deceptive. From a distance it looks almost gentle — rounded hills, a low spread of scrub and moss-green turf, cumulus clouds so white and volumetric they seem freshly made. But the ground beneath the horse's hooves is stone just below the surface. You feel every step transmitted upward through the saddle.

The bells on the harness rang with each stride, a small bright sound against the vast silence of the plateau. Red tassels swayed at the horse's chest. The woven blanket beneath the saddle was dense with colour — saffron, cobalt, deep crimson — the kind of colour that only makes sense against a background this austere. I remember thinking: the Tibetans understood something about contrast that the rest of us are still working out.

The cold was not brutal that day, but it was present — a steady insistence at the ears and the back of the wrists. The sun was strong, the UV at this elevation almost aggressive, and yet the wind periodically reminded you that warmth here is always conditional. Breathing remained the central task. Every slight incline required a conscious negotiation: slow down, breathe, let the body catch what the air is offering.

Behind me, others moved on foot across the same terrain, small figures against the bare hill that rose in a long smooth arc toward cloud. The prayer flags to the west snapped in the wind, their colours already faded from months of exposure. Nobody spoke much. That is the particular quality of high-altitude walking — it returns you to your own breath, your own heartbeat, your own footfall, or in this case the footfall of the animal carrying you.

## Inner Reflection — Through a Vedanta Lens

What the mountain does, slowly, is strip the narrative. You arrive with a story about who you are and why you came, and the altitude quietly dismantles it. Not dramatically — there is no thunderclap of insight — but incrementally, with each laboured breath, each step on stone, each moment in which the landscape refuses to be impressed by your presence.

Vedanta calls this process *viveka* — discernment — the gradual recognition of what is real from what is merely accumulated. Up here, the accumulated falls away faster. The self that plans and narrates and worries about its performance is simply too expensive to maintain when oxygen is scarce. What remains is something quieter. Not bliss. Just attention. The horse moves. The bells ring. The hill is the hill.

Kailash itself was not fully visible from this stretch of the western approach, hidden behind ridgeline and cloud. And somehow that felt appropriate. The sacred centre of the pilgrimage was present as an orientation, a direction, not a spectacle.

## A Sloka for the Path

**Sanskrit:** यदा विनियतं चित्तमात्मन्येवावतिष्ठते | निःस्पृहः सर्वकामेभ्यो युक्त इत्युच्यते तदा ||

**Transliteration:** Yadā viniyataṃ cittam ātmany evāvatiṣṭhate | niḥspṛhaḥ sarva-kāmebhyo yukta ity ucyate tadā ||

**Meaning:** When the mind, fully restrained, rests in the Self alone, free from longing for all objects of desire — then one is said to be in yoga.

**Reference:** Bhagavad Gita, Chapter 6, Verse 18

**Relevance:** On the kora, the stripping away of distraction is not metaphorical — it is physiological. The plateau enforces a kind of restraint the mind would not choose on its own. This verse does not describe an achievement so much as a condition that the mountain itself begins to create, step by laboured step.

## Practical Travel Notes

**Permits:** Foreign nationals require a Tibet Travel Permit, an Alien Travel Permit, and a Military Permit for the Kailash region. These must be arranged through a registered Chinese travel agency in Tibet — independent travel is not permitted. Allow at least three to four weeks for processing.

**Best season:** May through September. The window narrows sharply in either direction. July and August bring monsoon moisture from the south, but the trails remain passable. June is often considered the clearest month.

**Altitude preparation:** Lhasa sits at approximately 3,650 m. Spend a minimum of two to three nights there before proceeding. The Kailash base camp at Darchen is around 4,560 m, and Dolma La pass — the highest point of the kora — reaches approximately 5,636 m. Acclimatise seriously. Diamox is commonly used; consult a physician beforehand.

**Horses:** Available for hire at Darchen and along the route. Rates are negotiable. A horse covers the western and southern sections well; the northern approach to Dolma La is done on foot regardless.

**Duration:** The full kora takes most trekkers two to three days. Some complete it in one long day; this is not advisable for those unacclimatised.

## Closing Reflection

At some point in the afternoon the clouds moved and the light changed — that specific high-altitude light that is neither warm nor cold but simply very clear. The bells were still ringing. The hill behind me was exactly the same hill it had been for ten thousand years before I arrived and would be for ten thousand after.

I did not feel small in a diminishing way. I felt accurately sized, which is different. This place does not humble you by making you feel worthless. It humbles you by being indifferent, completely and serenely indifferent, and in that indifference offering something that feels, quietly, like relief.
