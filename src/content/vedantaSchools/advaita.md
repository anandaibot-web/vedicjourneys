Advaita — literally "not-two" — is the most widely studied school of Vedanta in the modern world. Where other philosophies ask "how do I relate to God?", Advaita asks a more radical question: "Is there any 'I' apart from the Absolute to relate?"

Shankara synthesised the scattered philosophical hints of the Upanishads into a rigorous, internally consistent system. He wrote commentaries on the ten principal Upanishads, the Bhagavad Gita, and the Brahmasutras — together called the *Prasthanatrayi*, the triple canon of Vedanta.

## The Three Levels of Reality

Advaita uses a three-tier ontology:

1. **Paramarthika** — the Absolute level: pure, undifferentiated Brahman-Consciousness. This alone is ultimately real.
2. **Vyavaharika** — the conventional level: the world we transact in. It is not unreal like a dream figure, but its apparent independence from Brahman is the error.
3. **Pratibhasika** — the illusory level: a rope mistaken for a snake, a dream, a hallucination.

The world is not nothing (shunya), nor is it a separate creation. It is Brahman appearing as diversity through the power of Maya — the same rope appearing as a snake.

## The Mahavakyas

Four great sentences (*mahavakyas*) from the Upanishads crystallise the Advaita vision:

- **Prajnanam Brahma** — Consciousness is Brahman (Aitareya Upanishad)
- **Aham Brahmasmi** — I am Brahman (Brihadaranyaka Upanishad)
- **Tat tvam asi** — That thou art (Chandogya Upanishad)
- **Ayam Atma Brahma** — This Self is Brahman (Mandukya Upanishad)

## The Path of Jnana

Advaita's primary path is *jnana yoga* — the yoga of discriminative inquiry. This involves three stages: **shravana** (hearing the teaching from a qualified teacher), **manana** (independent reasoning and removal of doubts), and **nididhyasana** (deep contemplation until the understanding becomes direct and unshakeable).

Preparatory disciplines — ethical living, equanimity, dispassion toward results — are not negotiable. Shankara insisted that Vedantic inquiry is not armchair philosophy but a complete reorientation of one's relationship with experience.
