Dvaita — "two-ness" or dualism — is the most theologically straightforward of the three great Vedanta schools. Madhva, a South Kanara Brahmin who is said to have been a wrestling champion before becoming a renunciant, brought a combative clarity to Vedantic debate. He rejected both Advaita and Vishishtadvaita as insufficiently honouring the absolute transcendence and supremacy of Vishnu.

His is a deeply personal theology: God is not an impersonal Absolute, not a substrate in which souls are embedded, but a living, loving Lord who is fully independent while souls and matter are eternally dependent on him.

## The Five-Fold Difference (Pancha Bheda)

Madhva's system rests on five irreducible distinctions:

1. **God ↔ Soul** — Vishnu and the individual jiva are distinct
2. **God ↔ Matter** — Vishnu and the material world are distinct
3. **Soul ↔ Soul** — each individual soul is distinct from every other
4. **Soul ↔ Matter** — souls and insentient matter are distinct
5. **Matter ↔ Matter** — each material entity is distinct from all others

These are not provisional distinctions that dissolve at the highest level of understanding. They are eternal, real, and constitutive of the nature of things.

## Taratamya: A Hierarchy of Souls

One of Madhva's distinctive contributions is *taratamya* — a graded hierarchy among liberated souls. Even in liberation, souls are not equal. Vayu (Mukhyaprana) occupies the highest position among souls; the Alvars, the teacher-saints, occupy exalted positions. This hierarchy is not a limitation but a rich structure of relationship, each soul worshipping according to its nature and capacity.

## The Grace of Vishnu

Liberation (*mukti*) in Dvaita is not the extinction of identity but its fullest flowering. The liberated soul retains its individuality, its distinct personality, and its love — and spends eternity in the blissful awareness of Vishnu's presence. "The highest joy is the direct vision of God, while knowing oneself as his servant."

This accessible, devotional entry point makes Dvaita — particularly through the Haridasa devotional tradition of Karnataka — a living practice for ordinary householders and monks alike.
