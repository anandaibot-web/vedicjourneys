  The four Vedas — Rigveda, Samaveda, Yajurveda, Atharvaveda — are the
  oldest stratum of Indian sacred literature. Transmitted orally for millennia
  before being written down, they are divided into Samhitas (hymns), Brahmanas
  (ritual commentaries), Aranyakas (forest texts), and Upanishads (the
  philosophical culmination). The Vedas are considered apaurusheya — not
  authored by any human, but "heard" (shruti) by seers (rishis) in states of
  deep meditation.

The Vedas are not a single book but a vast library transmitted across generations by specialised communities of memorisers whose accuracy was held to the syllable. Before the Vedanta schools emerged, the Vedas were primarily used as liturgical manuals — hymns to deities, fire sacrifices, incantations for rain, health, and victory.

Yet within this liturgical corpus, the seeds of the deepest philosophical questioning were already present. The Rigveda's famous Nasadiya Sukta (10.129) asks: "Who really knows? Who will here proclaim it? Whence was it produced? Whence is this creation? The gods came afterwards, with the creation of this universe. Who then knows whence it has arisen?"

That question — unanswered, deliberately left open — is the seed from which all Vedanta philosophy later grew.
