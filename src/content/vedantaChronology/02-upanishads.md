"Upa-ni-shad" literally means "sitting down near" — as a student sits near a teacher in the forest, in the intimate atmosphere where these dialogues were transmitted. The Upanishads mark a decisive inward turn: from sacrificing to the gods for worldly gains to inquiring into the nature of the one who sacrifices.

The Chandogya Upanishad contains one of the most celebrated teaching dialogues: Uddalaka Aruni instructing his son Shvetaketu through nine successive analogies — the salt dissolved in water, the banyan seed, the dream — each ending with the same refrain: *Tat tvam asi* — "That thou art." The Self you seek is not elsewhere. It is what you already are.

The Mandukya Upanishad, though the shortest (twelve verses), is considered by Advaita to contain the essence of all Vedanta. It analyses the three states of consciousness — waking, dreaming, deep sleep — and posits a fourth (*turiya*), which is not a state but the witnessing awareness underlying all three.

All three Vedanta schools — Advaita, Vishishtadvaita, Dvaita — regard the Upanishads as authoritative scripture and wrote detailed commentaries on them. The disagreements between the schools largely concern *how* to read the same Upanishadic verses.
