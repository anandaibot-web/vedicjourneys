Shankara was born in Kaladi (present-day Kerala). He renounced the world at eight, became a disciple of Govindapada on the banks of the Narmada, and by his early twenties had composed his most demanding work — the commentary on the Brahmasutras — which remains the locus classicus for all subsequent Advaita scholarship.

He walked to Varanasi, then south to Sringeri, north to Badrinath (where he meditated in the Himalayas), east to Puri, and finally to Kedarnath — where, according to tradition, he entered samadhi at thirty-two and was no more seen. In Sringeri, Puri, Dwaraka, and Badrinath, he established the four Shankaramathas, each charged with preserving the Advaita teaching.

What makes Shankara remarkable is not just the depth of his philosophy but its accessibility. Alongside his dense philosophical commentaries, he wrote the *Bhaja Govindam* — a short, musical hymn reminding his disciples that no amount of grammar-study will save them at the hour of death; only the recognition of Govinda, the inner Self, can. Philosophy and devotion were never separate in him.

His synthesis arrived at a precise moment: Buddhism had been the dominant intellectual force in India for centuries, and Shankara's engagement with Buddhist Madhyamaka and Yogachara philosophies — which he critiques and transforms — gave Vedanta a rigour it had not previously possessed. He is sometimes called, approvingly, a *pracchanna bauddha* — a hidden Buddhist — though the label misses how deeply his system differs in its affirmation of an absolute, conscious Brahman.
